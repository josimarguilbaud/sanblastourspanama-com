San Blas gets asked about more than most Caribbean destinations for one honest reason: it's remote, it involves open-water boat travel, and there's no quick way to leave once you're out there. Here's a straight answer.

## Boats and water safety

Life jackets are mandatory at all times on the water, for adults and children alike, and boats are operated by experienced local captains who know the reefs and channels. Guides throughout the trip are members of the Guna community with deep, practical knowledge of local conditions.

## Medical access and connectivity

There's no wifi on any island, and cell signal is inconsistent — some islands get a usable signal (Panamanian carriers Tigo and Más Móvil tend to work best), others don't. If a genuine medical emergency requires evacuation, operators cover it as part of their cancellation/refund policy, but it's worth knowing upfront that you're genuinely off-grid for the day (or days).

## Theft and personal belongings

San Blas has a low crime profile — the bigger practical risk is losing something in the sand or off a boat, not theft. Standard travel sense applies: keep valuables with you during day excursions, and secure them in your cabin during an overnight stay.

## Who shouldn't book this trip

Tour operators are consistent on this: San Blas is **not recommended** for pregnant travelers, people with back or hip conditions, or anyone with a serious medical condition that could be aggravated by rough terrain, sun exposure, or limited medical access. It's also **not wheelchair accessible** — expect short walks on sand and boat boarding/disembarking that requires some mobility.

## The bottom line

For a healthy traveler willing to embrace a genuinely remote, no-frills day (or few days), San Blas is a well-run, low-risk trip. The real preparation isn't about danger — it's about packing right, bringing cash, and knowing you'll be unreachable for a while.
